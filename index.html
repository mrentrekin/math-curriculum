Unit Overview
Students build and extend their understanding of the real number system ‚Äî distinguishing rational from irrational numbers, working fluently with integer exponents, and expressing extreme quantities using scientific notation. This unit develops the number sense that underpins all of algebra and provides students with tools used in science, engineering, finance, and everyday life.

  • How are square roots and cube roots connected to area and volume?

Card 1 of 10 ¬∑ Overview
Unit Overview
This is the algebraic heart of 8th grade mathematics. Students develop deep fluency with linear equations in one and two variables, moving from proportional relationships and slope to slope-intercept form and ultimately to systems of equations. Every concept is grounded in real-world contexts ‚Äî pricing comparisons, motion problems, business scenarios ‚Äî so students experience algebra as a powerful tool, not an abstract manipulation.

  • How can we use an equation to model a real-world linear relationship?
  • When two things change simultaneously, how do we find the point where they intersect?
  • What does it mean for a linear equation to have one solution, no solution, or infinitely many?

Card 1 of 9 ¬∑ Overview
Unit Overview
The functions unit is where algebra clicks. Students move beyond solving equations to understanding functions as input-output rules ‚Äî mathematical machines that transform inputs into outputs consistently and predictably. They learn to recognize, compare, and represent functions using four interchangeable lenses: equations, tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions. This representational fluency is the conceptual engine of all of high school mathematics.

Card 2 of 9 ¬∑ Essential Questions

  • How can the same function look completely different depending on how we represent it?
  • What can the shape of a graph tell us about the story of a situation?
  • How is a linear function different from a nonlinear one, and how can we tell them apart?

TermDefinition
FunctionA rule that assigns each input (x-value) exactly one output (y-value); no x-value maps to two different y-values
DomainThe set of all possible input values (x-values) of a function
RangeThe set of all possible output values (y-values) of a function

Nonlinear functionA function whose graph is curved; rate of change is NOT constant; examples: quadratic, exponential, absolute value

Card 1 of 11 ¬∑ Overview
Unit Overview
The geometry unit is one of the most visually rich and intellectually satisfying in 8th grade mathematics. Students explore transformations ‚Äî translations, reflections, rotations, dilations ‚Äî and discover the properties that are preserved or changed by each. They encounter the Pythagorean Theorem, perhaps the most famous result in all of mathematics, prove it, and apply it in two and three dimensions. The unit concludes with volume of cylinders, cones, and spheres, connecting geometric formulas to real-world design and measurement.

  • Why is a¬≤ + b¬≤ = c¬≤ always true for right triangles ‚Äî and how can we prove it?
  • How are parallel lines cut by a transversal connected to similar and congruent triangles?
  • How do the formulas for volume allow us to design and measure the real world?

Card 1 of 9 ¬∑ Overview
Unit Overview
Statistics and probability move math from the abstract to the immediately relevant. Students learn to collect, display, and interpret bivariate data ‚Äî data involving two variables ‚Äî through scatter plots and two-way tables. They find and interpret lines of best fit, distinguish between linear and non-linear associations, and reason about patterns, outliers, and the crucial difference between correlation and causation. These are skills students will use for the rest of their lives.

  • When two things seem related, does that mean one causes the other?
  • How do we use data to make predictions ‚Äî and how confident should we be in those predictions?
  • What can a two-way table tell us about patterns in categorical data?

CCSS Standards
StandardDescription
8.SP.A.1Construct and interpret scatter plots for bivariate measurement data to investigate patterns of association between two quantities. Describe patterns such as clustering, outliers, positive or negative association, linear association, and nonlinear association.
8.SP.A.2Know that straight lines are widely used to model relationships between two quantitative variables. For scatter plots that suggest a linear association, informally fit a straight line, and informally assess the model fit by judging the closeness of the data points to the line.

Learning Objectives
By the end of Unit 5, students will be able to:

Card 8 of 9 ¬∑ Assessment Philosophy
Assessment Philosophy
Assessment in this curriculum serves two purposes: to inform instruction (formative) and to document mastery (summative). The goal is never to catch students in failure but to create multiple opportunities to demonstrate what they know and to identify where they need support before that gap becomes permanent. A balanced assessment program includes daily, weekly, and unit-level data points ‚Äî and treats all of them as information, not just judgment.
